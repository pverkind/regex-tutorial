10. Matching a range of characters

The square brackets can also be used to specify a range of characters. For example, [0-9] will match any (Latin-script) digit, [a-z] will match any lower-case letter between a and z, and [A-Z] will match any upper-case letter between A and Z.

Regular expressions engines that are Unicode-compliant use the Unicode order of characters to determine which characters are included in a range; you can take a look at a Unicode chart to find the first and last character of your range. For example, the range [😀-🙏] will match any emoticon in the Unicode standard (see unicode-table.com/en/blocks/emoticons/).

These ranges can be combined with other characters or ranges: for example, the regex [a-fA-F.,] will match any letter from a to f (both lower- and upper-case), and any full stop or comma.

The caret "^" character can be used here as well to invert the options: [^a-z] will match any character that is not a lower-case letter from a to z.
