Regex tutorial for humanists

1. Literal characters: letters

Computers see text as a sequence of characters; in computer science, such a sequence of characters is referred to as a 'string' (the imagery is of characters as beads on a string, like a necklace). Everything in a text is considered a character in a string: not only letters and digits, but also whitespace (spaces, tabs, line breaks), punctuation and all other symbols you find on your keyboard.

In order to retrieve information from a string using regular expressions, we write patterns that match specific sequences of characters in that string. These patterns are also built up of characters. There are two types of characters in regular expressions:

  • metacharacters: characters that have a special meaning that differs from their usual meaning in regular text. There are only 14 of these special characters (we will discuss their special powers in the next lessons):
    {}[]()^$.|*+?\
  • all other characters in regular expressions are literal characters, and have their usual meaning: the character t in a regex (short for regular expression pattern) will match a lower-case letter "t" in a text string, the character ل in a regex will match the Arabic letter lām ("ل") in a text string, and so on.

NB: in the lessons in this tutorial, we will always highlight regular expressions in the lesson text in light grey, and put them in a different font and color, to distinguish them from regular text.

The simplest regular expression pattern consist entirely of literal characters.

Below you will find a number of sample text strings. In the search box below the sample strings, write a pattern that uses only literal characters to match the common word in all three lines.

As you start typing your pattern, you will see that all sequences of characters that match your pattern are highlighted (the color of the highlighting is alternating between green and blue to show separate matches).

A ✔ symbol will be displayed next to those sample strings your pattern matches correctly, and a ✘ sign will show you in which strings your pattern did not match the desired characters. Once your regex pattern correctly matches the desired characters in each string, you can move to the next lesson.
