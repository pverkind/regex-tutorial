4. Word and digit characters

We have seen that the backslash escapes the special powers of metacharacters and turns them into literal characters. The opposite happens when the backslash is put in front of some (but not all) literal characters: that character becomes a metacharacter with a special meaning.

For example, while the regex d will match the lower-case letter "d", \d stands for "any single digit character". Digit characters are defined in a variety of ways in different implementations of regular expressions; in most cases, \d will match only the Latin-script (ASCII) digits 0 to 9.

Similarly, the regular expression \w stands for "any single word character". Word characters are defined in a variety of ways in different implementations of regular expressions; in most cases, \w will match only any upper- and lower-case letter a to z and any digit from 0 to 9, in addition to the underscore ("_") character - that is, the characters that were allowed in variable names in the Perl computer language, which contained one of the earliest and fullest implementations of regular expressions.

The expression \W (that's a capital W) stands for the opposite: "any single character that is NOT a word character". Similarly, \D stands for "any single character that is NOT a digit".
