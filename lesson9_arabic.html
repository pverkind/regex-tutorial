Regex tutorial for humanists

9. Matching specific characters

Sometimes, it's not enough to state that any word character may be present; you want to specify which characters are allowed. We can use the square brackets [] for this. For example, the regex c[au]t will match "cat" and "cut" ('a "c" followed by either an "a" or a "u", followed by a "t"').

Sometimes, you also want to express the opposite: any character except X, Y or Z. To do this, you add the caret character "^" after the opening square bracket. For example, the regex la[^pgd] will match "lab" and "lax" but not "lap", "lag" and "lad".

Note that inside the square brackets all special characters except "\" and "]" lose their special powers: [.,] will match only a dot and a comma, not "any character except a new line". If you want to include a literal backslash or a literal closing square bracket among the options, you have to escape those: [[\]\\] will match either an opening bracket, a closing bracket or a backslash.
